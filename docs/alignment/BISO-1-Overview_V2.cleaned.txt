This white paper and its companion documents, Structuring a BISO Role for Success: Responsibilities, Organizational Approaches, and Performance Indicators in the Business Information Security Officer Role and Mapping the BISO Career: The Development of an Effective Business Information Security Officer, offer insight into the role design, value proposition, organization, responsibilities, and challenges of the Business Information Security Officer role.

The partnership across companies and industries demonstrates the value of collaboration on cyber defense. Threats cross departments, industries, and borders. Strengthening one sector’s cybersecurity and resilience ultimately strengthens all of them. 

Executive Summary
Cybersecurity incidents can disrupt customer services and business operations, impacting companies’ reputations and profitability. The evolving threat landscape and emerging technologies are accelerating the need to mature the security capabilities of business and technology teams and ensure a close partnership with cybersecurity. 
Many companies have implemented the Business Information Security Officer (BISO) role to help solve that challenge. The BISO acts as a liaison between business and cybersecurity, translating “security and compliance speak” into meaningful guidance and real-world recommendations. As a conduit between teams, BISOs reduce cyber risks, support business and compliance needs, and help companies manage risk and meet business objectives on time.
Though BISOs are becoming increasingly common, the responsibilities and requirements of the role and its programmatic implementation and structure are not yet standard across industries. Lack of definition makes it more difficult for companies to build effective BISO programs or optimize existing ones. To help businesses build – or build out – a BISO program, experts in over a dozen companies in four industries developed this series of three white papers. The documents detail the BISO function and offer a comprehensive examination of organizational structures, tactical advice, and lessons learned from real-world experience.
This Overview is industry-agnostic and designed for:
	•	Chief Information Security Officers (CISOs)/Chief Information Officers (CIOs)/Chief Risk Officers (CROs) considering the implementation of a BISO program or challenged with their program’s relationship with the business.
	•	C-level and other business executives faced with an ever-evolving cyber threat and risk landscape that requires division-specific oversight and direct partnership with cybersecurity.
	•	BISO leaders considering refinement of their BISO program.
	•	Cybersecurity professionals interested in assuming a BISO role.
The paper describes how BISOs’ in-depth knowledge of cybersecurity and alignment to the business allows organizations to take a more proactive approach to cybersecurity. It outlines the role, its responsibilities, its service catalog, and concludes with a step-by-step outline for building a BISO program. Its goal is to help companies – regardless of sector or level of cyber maturity – increase the security and resilience of their firms.

Scoping the BISO Role
A Business Information Security Officer can be thought of as a multi-directional conduit, uniting cybersecurity, business, and technology functions, providing the partnership and direction necessary to move quickly and manage risk. 
Smaller companies often have a single BISO. Big, complex, or highly regulated organizations — such as those in the healthcare or financial services sectors — often employ multiple BISOs aligned to a specific business/function/region (BFR) to ensure compliance and robust cybersecurity measures according to each BFR’s unique needs. Companies with operations in several jurisdictions and/or geographies are more likely to employ multiple BISOs as well.
BISOs often report to the CISO or liaise with the CISO’s representatives and other technology teams in support of BFRs. (More information on the structure and organizational hierarchy of the BISO role can be found in the second paper in this series, Structuring a BISO Role for Success: Responsibilities, Organizational Approaches, and Performance Indicators in the Business Information Security Officer Role.) BISOs must therefore have technical expertise – but to build trust between teams, they also need communication skills, executive presence, and relationship management abilities. 

BISO Responsibilities 
BISO responsibilities vary widely between business sectors but generally include: 
	•	Consulting and advising – The BISO consults on security controls and is responsible for ensuring cyber threats are properly mitigated. They often deliver cybersecurity-domain counsel — from answering questions to deep-dive engagements on key business and technology initiatives — to both business leaders and cybersecurity teams and translate and clarify the words, actions, and motives of each group. This is a substantial benefit of a BISO program. Cybersecurity functions are often very complex and highly specialized. They have their own jargon, objectives, and way of thinking about the world. Business teams have their own terminology, motivations, and accountabilities as well. When these differing outlooks clash, trust can be hindered and business and technology teams can be left frustrated and confused. By providing counsel, BISOs reduce complexity and facilitate secure business operations.
	•	Business cybersecurity accountability – BISOs align and engage with the business to foster knowledge of the people, objectives, challenges, and planned work that may affect cyber risk. The BISO shares responsibility with business unit leaders in ensuring compliance with cybersecurity policies, legal regulations, and oversight of the cybersecurity efforts that enhance both divisional and corporate security.
	•	BISOs don’t own the risk – they help define, communicate, escalate, and govern risk when necessary. Ultimately, the business owns the risk.

The BISO Program in Practice
A key BISO responsibility is developing recommendations and cybersecurity direction based on risk and business input. BISOs provide that expertise by collecting, processing, and analyzing data and information directly related to the business and making recommendations. This information drives improved decision-making in the specific threat landscape, people, processes, and systems related to the business. 
By implementing a BISO program, the risk profile of business teams can be escalated into the enterprise. Enterprises without BISOs tend to create programs, processes, and systems at the enterprise level, with little or no input from functions and business teams. As a result, they may lose important input from the broader organization.

The objective is to ensure the effectiveness of security resources while assisting the business in meeting its goals. The role of the BISO is not to slow down the business in favor of cybersecurity. BISOs help the business understand the risks, provide balanced decision-making, and avoid surprises. 
Building a BISO Program
The following approach to building a BISO program is developed by BISOs across industries. The steps are not necessarily sequential – some can be completed in tandem with one another – but the approach will help businesses develop an effective program. 
	•	Research the demand 
Survey/interview stakeholders to document and validate the need for a program and related services to address business gaps. 
	•	Identify the objectives 
Determine the business’ needs in order to define its objectives (e.g., improving customer satisfaction, enhancing cyber risk management, increasing uptake of security consulting offerings, etc.). 
The goals and objectives of the BISO program will be determined by its origin. For example, if the program originates from the CISO, then the BISO program will be cybersecurity-focused. If it originates from the business, the business will have more influence over the BISO program. 
	•	Gather data
Conduct surveys, interviews and questionnaires to gather direct feedback. Hold one-on-one interviews with key stakeholders for in-depth insights. Ask industry peers what is working in their organizations. 
Next, identify common themes and patterns in the feedback. Prioritize the needs based on frequency and impact.
Then translate the feedback into specific, actionable steps. Draft a plan to address the most critical needs first.
	•	Strategies to Gain Executive Commitment to a BISO Program
Requests for commitment to a BISO program/role should align with, augment, and elevate the business and cybersecurity mission, vision, and objectives. Where feasible, use a data-driven justification to supplement the request, highlighting how the BISO or more BISO resources will result in a return on investment (calculate the return as speed or time gained, costs reduced or avoided, compliance improved, etc.). Highlight the cost of inaction, as well as the real-world “wins” of competitors.

	•	Develop the framework

	•	Implement technology solutions
